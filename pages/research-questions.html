Questions to Ask Before You Try Peptides

Research these six areas before considering any peptide. Use this as a checklist with your provider.

1. Basic Understanding

  • What exactly is this peptide and how does it work for my goal (weight loss, healing, skin, etc.)?
  • Is it FDA-approved for any condition, or experimental/off-label?
  • Is it marketed correctly as a prescription drug, or sold as a “research chemical” or “supplement”?

2. Evidence and Expected Benefit

  • What human clinical studies exist for this peptide for my exact use (dose, duration, route, population)?
  • How big was the benefit and how long did it last after stopping?
  • Are there head-to-head comparisons with lifestyle change alone (nutrition, exercise, sleep)?

3. Safety, Side Effects, and Monitoring

  • What are common short-term side effects (nausea, headaches, injection reactions, fatigue)?
  • What are known or suspected long-term risks (cancer, hormone disruption, immune effects)?
  • Who should not use this (pregnancy, breastfeeding, cancer history, heart disease, autoimmune)?
  • How will safety be monitored (labs, vital signs) and how often?
  • What is the plan to stop or adjust if side effects appear?

4. Medical Supervision and Regulatory Status

  • Is my prescriber a licensed medical professional with real peptide therapy experience?
  • Is this use standard of care, off-label, or purely “experimental wellness”?
  • Is the clinic following FDA and state rules on compounding?

5. Product Quality and Sourcing

  • Is the peptide from an FDA-registered, compliant compounding pharmacy or an unknown vendor?
  • Can the supplier provide a Certificate of Analysis (purity, identity, sterility, endotoxin)?
  • Is it labeled for human prescription use, or “research use only / not for human consumption”?

6. Cost, Duration, and Alternatives

  • What is the full cost per month, including labs and follow-ups?
  • What happens when I stop—do benefits persist, or do symptoms usually return?
  • Have I already optimized lower-risk, lower-cost interventions (food, protein, fiber, sleep, stress, movement)?
  • What does my clinician recommend instead of peptides as first-line options?
